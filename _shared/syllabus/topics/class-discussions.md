# Class Discussions

Class discussions will play a very important role in your learning and writing for this class. My goal with the class discussions is to give you a chance to use the assigned readings in a semi-formal writing environment. Discussions can help solidify concepts and develop ideas for bigger writing projects. This page describes the expectations for class discussions.*

## Due Dates

- Your initial post is due by the deadline of the discussion.
- All response posts are due three days later.

## Expectations

**Initial Posts and Response Posts.** In order for a discussion to take place, you need to be formulating your own ideas and responding to others. For this reason, your work on these discussion boards will be evaluated in terms of your initial post and in terms of your response posts.

**Initial Posts** should be:

1. **Essays.** You should spend time digesting the assignment and the readings that inform the discussion prompt. Your post should reflect a greater level of thought and work than you would put into a casual email or chat response. You should think of your initial post as a mini-essay that helps you make a clear, focused point.
2. **Detailed.** Each of your initial posts should be **at least 200 words long**. Although I'm not worried about the difference between 190 and 210 words, a post that is half the required length does not demonstrate the amount of work you need to be doing with these assignments.
3. **Semi-formal.** This means that you should take some care with your writing. Spelling counts, as does organization and structure. But since your writing will take shape as part of a discussion, the level of formality will be somewhat different than for an essay that you would submit in a face-to-face class.
4. **Well-cited.** To earn full credit for most discussions, you will need to cite the assigned readings. This shows that you are working to apply the assigned readings to your writing. (Some assignments are not connected to an assigned reading. If that's the case, the assignment prompt will make it clear that citations are not necessary.)
5. **On time.** Initial posts submitted after the due date will be marked down considerably for lateness.

**Response posts** should be:

1. **Dialectic.** By that I mean that they should respond to the initial post with the intent of connecting with an idea and moving the discussion forward. This could take the form of a carefully thought out question, a (courteous) challenge, or an idea that moves the discussion in an unexpected direction.
2. **Grounded in the readings.** If you disagree with the initial poster, let them know, but your response must be drawn from the readings in the class. Your opinion counts, but it counts for much more if it's built upon the rest of the work we're doing in the class.
3. **Well-cited.** Like the initial posts, if you are using the words or thoughts of another author, you must cite them.
4. **Courteous.** We don't always have to agree, but we should be able to maintain a polite conversation and not resort to name calling or bullying.

You should **respond to at least 2 of the initial posts** in each discussion thread.

---

\*My approach to online class discussions is based in large part on the work of Warnock's *Teaching Writing Online*, Chapter 8. (Warnock, Scott. *Teaching Writing Online: How and Why*. Urbana, Ill: National Council of Teachers of English, 2009. Print.)
